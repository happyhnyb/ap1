Analysis of Myanmar's current agrifood landscape reveals a fascinating structural divergence between the rice & pulses markets and exports & domestic retail markets. The latest February 2026 data exposes a classic export-driven long term pricing mechanism and energy driven domestic retail inflation. Myanmar produces a massive rice surplus (11–12 MMT annually), exporting around 3 MMT. It also grows smaller volumes of pulses—such as green gram (1.1–1.2 MMT), black matpe (0.85–0.9 MMT), and pigeon peas (0.33–0.35 MMT)—but locals consume very little of them except green gram. Consequently, both sectors are structurally reliant on exports to clear inventory. Interestingly, the currently mentioned domestic retail prices due to high energy cost are trading higher than their FOB export equivalents. Because of this heavy export dependency, global market trends are effectively dictating local farm-gate realities: lower global prices are keeping rice domestic prices suppressed, while high global pulse prices are ensuring higher domestic prices for pulses. The Pulses Boom: Export-Driven Highs High global pulse prices are guaranteeing high domestic prices. Pigeon pea (tur) retail prices surged 7.0% year-on-year and 8.6% month-on-month to a staggering 3,224 MMK/KG. Black matpe (urad) climbed 2.1% month-on-month to 2,931 MMK/KG. While green gram (pedisein) remains highly elevated at 3,458 MMK/KG. This pricing power is entirely imported. India extended its duty-free import window after adverse weather impacted its domestic crop. Between April and January 2025-26, India imported

a massive 0.33 MMT of tur from Myanmar and 0.67 MMT of urad. Nearly the entirety of Myanmar’s two key pulse production is being absorbed by India, locking in highly lucrative domestic pricing. While 70% of exportable pedisein is procured by China. The Rice Reality: Squeezed by Global Supply Conversely, lower global rice prices are suppressing domestic rice prices. With India offloading a record rice harvest into the global trade stream, international benchmark prices have slumped. This global weight dragged Myanmar's national average retail price for Emata rice down 13.1% year-on-year to 1,498 MMK/KG. In major surplus-producing hubs like the Ayeyarwady region, prices have collapsed to just 987 MMK/KG. However, we are seeing some short-term resilience, with a slight 2.4% month-on-month price increase due to higher logistics cost. The Looming Macro Threat While pulse exporters are thriving, the broader agricultural producer is caught in the crosshairs of a major geopolitical threat. The conflict in the Middle East threaten to skyrocket imported fuel costs—a critical input for logistics and irrigation.
