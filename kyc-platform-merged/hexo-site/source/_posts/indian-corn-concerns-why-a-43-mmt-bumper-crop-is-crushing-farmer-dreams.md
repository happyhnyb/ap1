Indian agriculture is currently witnessing a massive structural paradox in the maize sector. We are staring at a bumper production mountain of 44+ million metric tons (MMT). Yet, instead of rural prosperity, this historic harvest is triggering severe farm-gate stress and a profoundly subdued mood heading into the crucial Kharif planting season. 1. The Acreage & Production Paradox📈 The push to expand maize cultivation from roughly 12 million hectares in the 24-25 season to a targeted 14 million hectares by 25-26 has successfully delivered a mammoth 10% increased harvest. However, achieving an agronomic victory without an aligned commercial exit strategy has created a severe domestic supply glut that the market is struggling to digest. 2. The Input Cost & Competitiveness Trap📉 Maize is a highly nutrient-intensive crop. Escalating input costs—from fertilizers to labor—have driven the fundamental Cost of Production to unsustainable highs. Because our baseline costs per ton are completely misaligned with global parity, Indian maize suffers from compromised global competitiveness. The result? Exports have collapsed to negligible, the domestic market bears the crushing weight of the entire surplus. 3. The Domestic Demand Squeeze (The Ethanol Bottleneck)⛽ The industry largely banked on the biofuel sector to act as a robust shock absorber for excess supply. Oil Marketing Companies (OMCs) have aggressively pivoted to grain, allocating a staggering 4.79 billion liters of ethanol from maize for the Ethanol Supply Year (ESY) 2025-26. This makes maize the dominant biofuel feedstock, representing over 45.7% of the total 10.5

billion liter OMC tender, secured at ₹71.86/litre. However, this is where the bottleneck hits. The capped national ethanol blending targets mean industrial demand cannot scale to absorb the remaining bumper crop. 4. The Fading MSP Safety Net🛡️ The Minimum Support Price (MSP) exists to protect farmers from precisely this scenario of oversupply. For the 2025-26 season, the maize MSP is pegged at ₹24,000 per ton. In reality, the MSP chasm is fading into irrelevance. Because there is an absence of the large-scale, robust government procurement machinery that exists for wheat and paddy, the declared MSP offers virtually zero tangible price support on the ground. Farmers are left entirely at the mercy of distressed open-market dynamics. The Kharif Outlook: A Looming Acreage Shift?🌱 You cannot expect farmers to continually absorb the high financial risk of a nutrient-heavy, capital-intensive crop without a functional safety net or viable export markets. The current price realization of ₹17,000 per ton sits at the point of a collapsed farmer dream. We must anticipate a significant recalibration of planted acreage.
