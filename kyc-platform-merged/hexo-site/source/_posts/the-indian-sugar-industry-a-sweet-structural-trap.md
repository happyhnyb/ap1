The projections for the 2025-26 Sugar Season describe a sector undergoing a disturbing structural shift. The numbers signal a move from global dominance to a "managed" domestic retreat. Since 2021-22, Gross Production is projected to drop from 39.3 million tons (MMT) to 31.4 MMT (a -20.1% decline), while Total Availability has plummeted by -24.8% to 33.3 MMT. Most critically, India’s footprint in the global market is vanishing; Exports are set to collapse by -93.6%, falling from 11.0 MMT to a mere 0.7 MMT (against quota of 2 MMT). This inventory squeeze is further reflected in Closing Stocks, which have dwindled by -42.4% to 4.1 MMT compared to 7.1 MMT in 2021-22. The "Soybeanization" Trap This trajectory is a mirror image of the Soybean Cycle: years of flat productivity where growth only comes from area expansion while yields and recovery rates remain stagnant at 1/3 of global average. While global competitors achieve cane yields of 120 t/ha and recovery rates of 14–16%, India remains trapped at 75 t/ha and ~10%, hampered by obsolete machinery and a lack of varietal innovation. We are becoming hostages of inefficient productivity, creating structural imperfections with heavy dependency on government support a permanent, "fixed" feature of 540 odd millers' balance sheet. The Ethanol Mirage: A "Push" Without a "Pull" The industry’s savior was supposed to be the Ethanol Blended Petrol (EBP) Programme. While ethanol economics work in high-crude crises (like today’s $100+ Brent), the "Business as Usual" scenario reveals a fragile foundation. If crude returns to its historical mean of $60/bbl, the economic value of ethanol begins to look like melting ice. With the Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP) at ₹355/qtl, the cost of production remains high (₹70/litre), leaving zero "pull" from Oil

Marketing Companies. Ethanol remains a product of government push—a mandated survival tactic rather than a market-led solution. Is FRP Stifling Innovation? The FRP has inadvertently created an innovation barrier. When cane prices are disconnected from sugar realizations (effective cane cost is ₹35.1/kg of sugar) and byproduct value, mills are left with zero surplus for R&D. We have effectively subsidized inefficiency loosing global competitiveness. The industry has become dole-dependent, constantly lobbying rather than innovating. The Verdict: Deregulation Over Doles The Indian sugar industry doesn't need doles; it needs radical deregulation: Revenue Sharing: Link farmer payments to market realities via a Revenue Sharing Formula (RSF). Radial Reform: End the 15km distance barrier to allow healthy competition and fresh investment. Modernization: Large-scale varietal replacement and mechanization. Without these reforms, the industry will remain a structural laggard—surviving on the "push" of policy rather than the "pull" of the market.
