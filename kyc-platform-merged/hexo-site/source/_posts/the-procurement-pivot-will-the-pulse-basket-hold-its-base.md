The Indian pulses market is navigating a high-stakes tug-of-war between a record-breaking harvest and the government’s procurement machinery. With mandi arrivals hitting peak momentum across the heartland, the "procurement window" has transitioned from a mere safety net to the primary architect of the price floor. The market is currently deciphering whether the government's stance will be aggressive enough to mop up the surplus or lukewarm, leaving farmers at the mercy of a supply-heavy open market. Tur (Pigeon Peas): The Resilience of the New Base Tur remains the most resilient player in the pulses basket, anchored by the government's 100% procurement commitment under the Aatmanirbharta Mission. In key hubs like Akola (Maharashtra) and Gulbarga (Karnataka), wholesale prices are currently hovering between ₹7,940 and ₹8,180 per quintal. With the MSP set at ₹8,000, the market is effectively trading at or slightly above par. This suggests that the government’s guarantee and anticipated tur short crop is successfully acting as a psychological floor. However, for this stability to hold throughout the harvest, state agencies must maintain an active buying presence to absorb early high-moisture arrivals that private millers typically discount. Chana (Desi Chickpeas): The High-Volume Battleground Chana is the most vulnerable to volume shocks this season, driven by a massive 13.5 MMT production estimate. In the mandis of Madhya Pradesh—including Indore, Ashta, and Gwalior— wholesale rates are currently averaging between ₹5,250 and ₹5,650 per quintal, marginally below the MSP of ₹5,875. This gap is the critical zone. The government has signaled an aggressive target of 2.3 MT to rebuild depleted national buffers. If NAFED and NCCF accelerate their "vacuum" operation now, prices will likely recover toward the MSP. If procurement remains

lukewarm or bogged down by logistics, the impending April arrival peak could drive prices down toward the ₹5,100 level. Masoor (Lentil): The Red Zone Masoor is currently facing the sharpest downward pressure relative to its support price. In Uttar Pradesh (Lalitpur, Bareilly, Kanpur), whole raw Masoor is trading in the ₹6,100–₹6,450 range, significantly trailing the ₹7,000 MSP. Despite a record production year, the lack of a secondary buyer of scale beyond the government makes this crop highly sensitive. While procurement has begun in pockets, the market in UP is desperate for a massive, aggressive entry by state agencies to prevent a post-harvest price rout. The Bottom Line The pulses market is at a strategic crossroads. An aggressive procurement over the next 60 days will set a higher base for the 2026-27 season and reinforce farmer confidence in the "Aatmanirbharta" goals.
