The geopolitical map of 2026 has been redrawn in less than 14 days. As the U.S.-Israel-Iran conflict paralyzes the Strait of Hormuz—the world’s most critical energy artery—the Russian economy is experiencing a structural resurgence that few predicted at the start of the year. What we are witnessing is not just a price spike; it is a fundamental re-valuation of non-Hormuz energy assets. 1. The "Urals Premium" Paradox For two years, the market’s focus was on the "G7 Price Cap" and the "Russian Discount." That era is over. The discount has flipped to a strategic premium. The Price Surge: Brent crude has blasted past the $120/barrel mark once and is consistently above $90, up from $70 in February. Historic Reversal: Russian Urals crude, which previously traded at a $10–15 discount to Brent, is now commanding a $4–$5 premium for Indian and Asian deliveries. With the Persian Gulf effectively "locked," Russian barrels are the only high-volume, "safe-route" alternative available. Budget Windfall: Russia’s 2026 budget was built on a conservative $59/bbl assumption. At current realizations of $90–$95/bbl, the Kremlin is looking at a massive fiscal surplus, with daily fossil fuel revenues already up 7–13% month-on-month. 2. Europe’s "LNG Trap" and the Policy Rethink Europe’s strategy to decouple from Russia relied heavily on Qatari LNG. With the Strait of Hormuz closed, that strategy has hit a wall. The Qatari Shortfall: Roughly 20% of global LNG is currently trapped. The Rethink: European gas storage levels are at a 3-year low (46 bcm). Industry analysts now suggest that Europe may be forced to quietly "re-examine" its anti-Russia energy policy. The

perceived reliability of Russian pipeline gas (via the Druzhba or TurkStream) is suddenly outweighing the "Risk Tax" of a frozen, LNG-starved winter. 3. The Fertilizer & Agri-Export "Turbo" The impact extends far beyond the oil well. Russia is leveraging the "Input Cost Crisis" to solidify its role as the world's primary nutrient supplier. The Urea Shock: Global Urea prices have breached $600/MT, a 50% jump this year. With Middle Eastern plants (Qatar, Iran, Egypt) struggling with gas feedstock disruptions, Russia has stepped in to fill the gap for Brazil, India, and the U.S. Grain Realization: Russian wheat and oilseed exports are seeing higher realizations as global logistics costs make long-haul shipping from Australia or North America prohibitively expensive. Analysis: The Decoupling of the "War Machine" In early March, the US Treasury issued a 30-day waiver for Indian refiners to purchase Russian oil stranded at sea—a tacit admission that global stability now requires Russian volume to prevent a $150 oil shock. For the Russian economy, the "Hormuz Risk" is the ultimate insurance policy.
