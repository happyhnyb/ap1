The "war premium" has officially arrived at the Indian doorstep. As of this morning, March 7, 2026, the government has announced a sharp upward revision in Liquified Petroleum Gas (LPG) prices. Domestic cylinders are up by ₹60, while commercial cylinders have seen a massive jump of ₹115. For the Indian consumer and the broader economy, this isn't just a kitchen-budget issue; it is a signal of a deepening energy crisis triggered by the ongoing conflict in the Middle East and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The Domino Effect: Why Petrol and Diesel are Under Watch As an economist, the question isn't if petrol and diesel prices will follow, but when and by how much. India is currently navigating a high-stakes energy "pincer movement": The $90 Crude Reality: With global benchmarks hitting $90/barrel due to the US-Iran conflict, the under-recoveries for Oil Marketing Companies (OMCs) are reaching a breaking point. The Currency Weight: With the Rupee breaching the 92 level against the USD, the cost of every imported barrel has effectively increased by nearly 8–10% in local terms over the last week alone. Logistical Nightmares: The diversion of tankers via the Cape of Good Hope adds not just time, but significant freight and insurance surcharges. These costs are traditionally passed on to the pump to prevent a fiscal deficit blowout. The Strategic Dilemma for the Government The government is caught between a rock and a hard place. Holding prices steady would require a massive increase in subsidies, further straining a fiscal deficit already under pressure from

war-driven fertilizer costs. Conversely, raising petrol and diesel prices now would ignite a fresh wave of logistics-led inflation, affecting everything from food to manufacturing. What Should Businesses and Households Prepare For? Short-Term Volatility: Expect a "calibrated" increase in fuel prices. The government may look to slash excise duties partially to cushion the blow, but the sheer scale of the crude spike makes a retail price hike almost inevitable by mid-March. Logistics Surcharges: For those in the agrifood and manufacturing sectors, the commercial LPG hike will immediately increase processing and transport costs. The 94-Level Rupee: As fuel demand stays inelastic, the massive dollar outflow to pay for oil will keep the INR under intense pressure, likely heading toward the 94 level in the short term. Conclusion: The End of Cheap Energy? The era of predictable fuel pricing has been suspended. The current LPG hike is the first "smoke signal" of a broader inflationary fire. Until the geopolitical dust settles in the Gulf, the Indian economy must brace for a high-cost energy regime.
