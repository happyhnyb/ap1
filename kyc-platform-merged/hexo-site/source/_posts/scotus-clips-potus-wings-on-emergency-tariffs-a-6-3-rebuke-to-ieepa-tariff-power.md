Today (Feb 20, 2026), the U.S. Supreme Court delivered a landmark 6–3 decision striking down the core of President Donald Trump’s sweeping “global/reciprocal” tariffs that were imposed using the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) of 1977. The Court’s message is blunt: IEEPA may be an emergency economic powers statute, but it is not a blank cheque to rewrite tariff schedules. Tariffs are taxes. And in the U.S. constitutional design, the primary tariff lever sits with Congress —not the Oval Office. What changes immediately The broad, across-the-board tariffs justified through IEEPA are invalidated. Tariffs imposed under other legal routes (think national-security and trade-law tools) are not automatically touched—meaning the tariff map does not go to “zero,” it gets re-routed. The real market story: remedies + refunds The next battlefield is not ideology, it’s accounting. Importers will now press for duty recovery. Estimates suggest well over $175B of tariff revenue could be at risk of refund depending on how remedies are structured and how quickly claims move through the system. Implications for business strategy Policy volatility doesn’t vanish—it changes form. Future tariffs will likely shift toward statutes with more process (investigations, findings, timelines), making them slower but potentially more durable. Contracting needs a legal-risk layer: tariff pass-through clauses, renegotiation triggers, and documentation discipline will matter as much as hedging.

Supply chains will re-price: some categories could see relief; others may face “replacement tariffs” via alternative authorities. Trade policy remains a strategic weapon. Today’s ruling simply tightens who can pull the trigger—and how.
