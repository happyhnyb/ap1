The narrative of the "Beijing summit" is shifting from a scheduled diplomatic reset to a volatile theater of leverage. While President Trump uses the summit as a carrot to force maritime cooperation, Beijing’s position is far from weak. In fact, the current "Hormuz Waiver" and China’s dominant grip on critical supply chains have turned this into a contest of who can withstand a global market freeze the longest. 1. The Waiver Illusion: China’s Energy "Safe Passage" While the U.S. frames the Strait of Hormuz as a chokepoint to pressure Xi, the reality on the water is different. The "Blanket Waiver": Iran has validated a standing "Safe Passage" agreement for Chinese and Russian-flagged tankers. While the rest of the world pays "War-Risk" premiums and reroutes around the Cape, Chinese crude continues to flow through the Strait virtually unimpeded. The Strategic Buffer: Because China isn't facing the same shipping "tax" as U.S. allies, Beijing views Trump’s demand for naval intervention not as a necessity for their own survival, but as a U.S. request to do Washington’s "heavy lifting." For Xi, there is no immediate "energy panic" to force a submissive stance at the summit. 2. Tariffs vs. The "Tech-Metal" Shield The U.S. administration frequently uses "Phase 3" tariffs as a bargaining chip, but Beijing has long moved past a defensive posture on trade. The Rare Earth Leverage: China’s counter-leverage isn't just about absorbing tariffs; it’s about the

ability to choke the U.S. defense and high-tech sectors. With a 90% dominance in processed rare earth elements and a vice-grip on the global battery supply chain, Beijing views U.S. tariffs as a manageable cost compared to the strategic damage they can inflict on U.S. industrial capacity. The Supply Chain Stalemate: If the summit is postponed and a "Tariff Snap-Back" occurs, Beijing’s response is likely to be a targeted restriction on critical components. In this scenario, the U.S. risks "Inflationary Suicide". 3. The Postponement Paradox: Who Blinks First? President Trump’s threat to delay the summit until China "helps" with Hormuz is a high-stakes gamble on the U.S. domestic economy’s resilience. A U.S.-China "truce" removes the last remaining hope for a market-calming event. If the summit is pushed to Q3, it signals to the world that the two largest economies are comfortable with a "Long Trade War". The Bottom Line: A Stalemate of Giants Trump is betting that he can trade trade-relief for China’s naval power in the Gulf. Xi is betting that China’s "Hormuz Waiver" and supply-chain dominance make the U.S. more desperate for a deal than Beijing.
