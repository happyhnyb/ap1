The global commodity landscape in early 2026 is defined by a structural repricing of risk. An escalating Middle East conflict has transformed the Strait of Hormuz into a critical bottleneck, injecting a powerful geopolitical premium into crude oil and the wider agricultural complex. This shock is transmitted with force into vegetable oils and fertilizers, reshaping price dynamics for the 2026–27 crop cycle. Crude Oil: From Risk to Repricing Brent crude has surged into triple digits, trading in the low-$110s per barrel in mid-March, roughly 55% higher than last month. The late February military escalations effectively choked a corridor handling over 15 million barrels per day. While prices briefly consolidated near $90, persistent shipping interruptions and insurance dislocations have re-ignited bullish sentiment. Forward curves now cluster in the $110–$115 range, repricing the Middle East risk as a semi- permanent feature of the 2026 landscape. Soybean Oil: Biodiesel as a Transmission Channel This energy volatility has established a direct transmission into agriculture via the biodiesel value chain. As petroleum-based diesel rallies, biofuel profitability expands, intensifying competition for feedstocks like soybean oil. In this regime, soybean oil acts as a high-beta surrogate for crude, with correlations exceeding 0.8. Recent action underscores this: US soybean oil futures are trading in the mid-60s cents per pound. When Brent broke $100 in early March, soybean oil responded with sharp intraday gains. Implied premiums suggest a plausible upside toward the mid-70s cents if crude holds current

levels. High fossil diesel prices mechanically pull vegetable oil into fuel channels, creating a demand floor anchored in the energy complex rather than pure food demand. The “Three Fs”: Fuel, Fertilizer, and Finance The conflict has unleashed secondary pressures across the “Three Fs”. Natural gas benchmarks have moved higher, while nitrogen fertilizer markets confront elevated feedstock costs. Key granular urea benchmarks have gained 30% or more, driving up the cost of production per hectare. If the Hormuz impairment persists, global fertilizer benchmarks could average 15–20% above pre-crisis levels through H1 2026. Tighter, more expensive credit further amplifies the risk of undersupplied markets if farmers scale back planted area due to rising costs. Conclusion Soybean oil has emerged as the primary price-discovery vehicle for the soy complex. As long as a geopolitical premium remains embedded in energy, it will maintain its role as a high-beta expression of the energy trade.
