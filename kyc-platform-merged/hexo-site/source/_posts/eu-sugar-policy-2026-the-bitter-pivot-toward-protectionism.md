As we move through March 2026, the European Union is signaling a seismic shift in its sugar trade architecture. Commissioner Christophe Hansen's announcement that the European Commission intends to suspend duty-free sugar imports for at least one year is more than just a regulatory tweak—it is a strategic intervention to rescue a domestic sector struggling under the weight of a global supply glut and collapsing regional prices. 1. EU Production: A Surplus in Search of a Floor The EU’s 2025–26 sugar beet harvest has been surprisingly robust. Favorable weather in the "Sugar Belt" (France, Germany, and Poland) has pushed production estimates toward 15.8 MMT, a 6% increase year-on-year. However, this bounty has been a double-edged sword. With domestic stocks high, the influx of duty-free sugar has sent white sugar prices in the EU plummeting from their 2024 peaks of €800/t to below €450/t in some regions, threatening the solvency of smaller beet processors. 2. The 700,000-Tonne Curb: Stabilizing the Market The core of the Commission’s plan is to remove roughly 700,000 tonnes of "preferential" sugar from the internal market. The Logic: By suspending duty-free access, the EU is effectively raising a "financial wall" that forces industrial users (confectionery and beverage giants) back toward local beet sugar. The Goal: To lift regional prices back to a "sustainable corridor" of €550–€600/t, ensuring that beet contracts for the 2026 planting season remain attractive to farmers. 3. Tentative Duties: What Can Be Invoked? While the exact "emergency brake" triggers are being finalized, analysts expect the EU to revert

to Most Favoured Nation (MFN) rates or specific SPS (Special Safeguard) duties. MFN Rates: We could see a return to the full duty of €419/t for white sugar and €339/t for raw sugar for countries whose Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) are suspended. Volume Triggers: The suspension will likely be automated based on "Traditional Trade Flows," where any volume exceeding a 3-year average is slapped with a prohibitive tariff. 4. The Impact on Key Exporters This move sends shockwaves through the EU’s traditional suppliers: Ukraine: The most impacted. As a major source of duty-free sugar since 2022, Ukraine’s exports to the EU will now face a hard ceiling, forcing them to find outlets in North Africa or the Middle East. Brazil: Although Brazil primarily trades on the world market, the EU's withdrawal as a high- priced sink for surplus white sugar will marginally increase competition in the "London No. 5" futures market. Strategic Takeaway The EU is moving from a "Trade First" to a "Farmer First" posture in 2026.
