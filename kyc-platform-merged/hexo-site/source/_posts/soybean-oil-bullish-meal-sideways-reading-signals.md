The soy complex in 2026 is no longer moving in unison. CBOT price structure, open interest trends, and demand composition all point toward a clear divergence—soybean oil supported by structural pull, while soybean meal remains range-bound under cyclical pressures. CBOT snapshot: firm beans, capped meal: CBOT soybean futures for March 2026 are trading near 1,043 cents/bushel, with forward contracts gradually firming toward ~1,070 cents into mid-2026–27, indicating a mildly supportive but not explosive bean structure. Soybean meal futures tell a different story. Front-month values sit roughly in the $285–$300/short-ton band, with deferred contracts only modestly higher near $305–$310, reflecting limited upside momentum despite normal seasonal volatility. This flat forward curve is the clearest evidence of a sideways fundamental narrative for meal. Oil strength visible in positioning: While exact daily prices fluctuate, open-interest expansion in CBOT soybean oil futures— jumping sharply to ~27,780 contracts in early February 2026—signals growing speculative and commercial conviction around tighter oil fundamentals. Rising participation during a period of policy-driven biofuel demand reinforces the thesis that oil is becoming the primary margin carrier in the crush equation. Short-term dynamics (0–3 months): Beans stable around $10.40/bu equivalent, reflecting balanced global supply. Meal confined to mid-$280s to low-$300s/ton, with rallies capped by substitution and comfortable

protein supply. Oil attracting stronger positioning and volatility due to energy linkage and tightening balance sheets. Result: oil reacts sharply to news; meal fades rallies. Medium-term structure (6–9 months): Forward CBOT curves show gradually firmer soybean prices, not a supply-shock bull run. Only incremental gains in meal, confirming structural ceiling from feed economics. At the same time, geopolitical trade signals—such as expectations of large Chinese purchases supporting futures—can lift sentiment but do not fundamentally tighten meal supply. Thus, oil demand linked to renewable fuels becomes the decisive medium-term driver, not livestock expansion. Strategic interpretation for the soy complex: 1. Beans are stable, not explosive. 2. Meal is capped by substitution and efficiency. 3. Oil is gaining structural leadership through energy demand and positioning. In essence, the soy complex is splitting into two different commodities sharing one raw material: Soy oil energy-linked, structurally bullish.→ Soy meal feed-linked, cyclically sideways.→ For crushers, traders, and policymakers, this means the future of soy profitability will increasingly be decided in fuel markets not feed markets. This single shift may redefine global oilseed economics for the next decade.
