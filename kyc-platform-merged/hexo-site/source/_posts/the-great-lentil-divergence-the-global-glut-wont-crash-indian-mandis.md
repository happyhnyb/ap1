The global agricultural stage is currently playing host to a fascinating paradox. On one side, we have a "mountain of masoor (lentils)" building up; on the other, a domestic Indian market that refuses to blink. If you’re looking at headline global prices and expecting a dip in Indian mandis, you’re missing the structural "ballast" keeping our local markets steady. The 2025–26 Masoor season is defying the gravity of a global surplus. The Global "Mountain of Masoor" The numbers out of the export hubs are staggering. Canada is sitting on a total production of roughly 3.4 million tons (MMT), with red lentils alone accounting for 1.8 MMT. Combine that with Australia’s robust forecast of 1.9 MMT, and you have a global red lentil pool of nearly 3.7 MMT. In a vacuum, this volume would trigger a price freefall. Indeed, we see this reflected in current global offers, with CFR India values hovering around $590 USD/MT. However, the world of commodities doesn’t exist in a vacuum—it exists in a landscape of logistics and currency. The Currency and Freight "Tax" The $590 global price is effectively a mirage for the Indian importer. With the USD to INR exchange rate sitting at 94, the currency depreciation acts as a massive hidden cost. When you layer on the current 10% import duty the landed cost of imported lentils climbs to approximately ₹61,000 per ton. The "Currency and War Tax" means that even as prices at the origin (Canada and Australia) weaken due to the glut, the cost of bringing that supply to an Indian warehouse remains high enough to provide a protective buffer for our farmers.

The Domestic Anchor: Desi Premium & MSP In India, the narrative is driven by quality and policy. The new domestic crop is hitting the mandis at a time when the government has set a firm floor with a ₹70,000/ton MSP. While raw mandi prices are currently floating between ₹63,000 and ₹65,000 per ton, we must account for the "Desi Premium." Domestic Masoor traditionally commands a 5% premium over imported varieties due to milling quality and consumer preference. The 10% discount on MSP also keeps the government interested in building a buffer. The Outlook: Firm vs. Faded As we look ahead, expect a "dual-speed" market: Domestic Masoor: Likely to remain firm, gravitating toward the ₹6,700–₹6,900 range. The combination of the Desi premium and the psychological safety net of the MSP makes a deep correction unlikely. Imported Masoor: Likely to remain range-bound or weak. The global glut ensures there is no shortage of supply, but the high landed costs (FX/Freight) prevent aggressive merchant buying. Imports will act as a "ceiling," preventing domestic prices from spiking too high, but they won't have the momentum to pull the market down.
