India’s ethanol programme is no longer at a stage where minor quota tweaks can solve structural stress. The recent appeals—from sugar cooperatives seeking higher diversion quotas to grain- producing states demanding larger procurement allocations—are not competing demands. They are warning signals. The present allocation-driven system is stretching both sugar mills and grain ethanol producers simultaneously. The only durable solution now is bold and clear: commit to E30 by 2030, rationalise ethanol pricing across feedstocks, and enable ethanol exports with calibrated incentives. India today faces a paradox. Sugarcane production remains strong, yet sugar mills are financially strained due to high cane prices and limited export competitiveness. At the same time, grain- based ethanol capacity has expanded aggressively, particularly in states like Bihar, where production exceeds allocated procurement. Oil marketing companies are navigating between feedstocks under an administered pricing framework that distorts incentives rather than optimising economics. The result is predictable—surplus potential, idle capacity, liquidity stress, and farmer anxiety. Accelerating the blending roadmap to E30 by 2030 transforms this tension into opportunity. Higher blending immediately expands structural domestic demand, absorbing additional ethanol from both sugar and grain routes. Instead of feedstocks competing for a fixed quota, they participate in a growing energy market. This strengthens energy security, reduces crude import dependency, and creates stable demand visibility for farmers and processors alike. However, higher blending alone will not suffice without price rationalisation. Current differential pricing between sugar-based and grain-based ethanol is skewing procurement

behaviour and capital allocation. A move toward parity-based pricing, anchored to energy equivalence and production efficiency, will restore neutrality. Investment decisions should reflect productivity and cost efficiency and capital incentives for new production facilities need to be stopped—not policy distortions. Rational pricing will also protect fiscal stability and prevent long-term subsidy burdens. A third, strategic lever lies in India’s significant rice surplus and expanding maize production. Channeling excess foodgrain into ethanol production is further leading to market distortions. Yet as blending rises toward E30, production capacity may still outpace domestic absorption during bumper crop years. This is where policy maturity is required. Allowing ethanol exports, supported by targeted incentives when necessary, creates a safety valve. The alternative—continuing with administrative quota management—will neither protect farmers sustainably nor encourage disciplined industrial growth. The strategic choice is clear a coherent architecture for India’s biofuel future.
