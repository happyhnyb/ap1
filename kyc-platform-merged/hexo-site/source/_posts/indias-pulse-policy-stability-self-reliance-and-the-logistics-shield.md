As we approach the fiscal deadline on March 31, 2026, the corridors of power in New Delhi are signaling a "steady-as-she-goes" approach to India’s vital pulse trade. The Government of India is likely to maintain its current import architecture, a sophisticated balancing act that aims to keep consumer prices in check while ensuring the domestic farmer captures the "Premium" they deserve in a high-inflation world. The Production Cushion: Abundance Meets Resilience The statistical backbone of this policy is the 2nd Advance Estimates for the 2025–2026 crop cycle. The numbers paint a picture of a "comfortable" supply side. Tur (Pigeon Peas) production is estimated at 3.45 MMT, signaling a healthy recovery in the Kharif belts. Gram (Chickpeas) continues to be the anchor of India’s food security at a robust 11.79 MMT, while Lentils (Masoor) hold steady at 1.73 MMT. This domestic cushion allows the government to resist reactive policy shifts, providing the market with the predictability it has craved since the 2022 volatility. The Validated Duty Regime: Decoding the Post-March 31 Landscape The upcoming policy cycle is set to validate a multi-tier duty structure. Tur and Urad will remain on the "Duty-Free" list, a move essential for maintaining the supply as we continue to lean on key global origins. Conversely, Lentils and Desi Chickpeas will continue to attract a 10% Basic Customs Duty (BCD), a strategic floor designed to prevent domestic prices from crashing during the peak harvest arrival. Yellow Peas, which saw a policy flip in late 2025 to prevent market flooding, will maintain a 30% duty, while Kabuli Chickpeas will remain at a steeper 40% duty to

protect local high-value growers. Moong Beans remain strictly in the "Restricted" category to protect the margins of local growers. The "Logistics Shield": Why Freight is the New Tariff In 2026, the most potent protection for the Indian farmer isn't always a government tariff—it’s the "Risk Tax" on global shipping. With the Red Sea disruptions persisting, the cost of bringing a container from Western origins like Canada, the USA or Brazil has surged by 15–20%. The necessity of rerouting vessels around the Cape of Good Hope adds roughly $1 million per voyage in fuel and insurance surcharges. These staggering logistical costs act as a "natural barrier," keeping the landed price of imported pulses high. This ensures that domestic harvest can be sold at reasonable price without the need for the government to impose further restrictive duties. Strategic Takeaway: Supply is Adequate, but the Pipeline is Expensive The 2026 pulse policy is a masterclass in "Passive Protectionism."
