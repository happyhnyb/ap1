The new desi chickpeas (chana) season has begun to unfold across key mandis in Andhra Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, and Maharashtra, and the early signals are instructive. Prices are currently scattered across a wide band, largely reflecting differences in moisture and lot quality rather than any structural tightness in supply. As is typical in the early harvest window, the market is still in the process of true price discovery. What stands out this year is that the broader supply backdrop—both domestic and global— appears comfortable. Early arrivals, though uneven, are expected to gather pace over the coming weeks. At the same time, competing pulse supplies globally remain adequate, and there is no visible trigger at present that would justify a sustained sharp rally in desi chana. This suggests that the market is more likely to settle into a range-bound pattern rather than a strong bullish trend through the peak arrival phase. In this environment, procurement strategy must prioritise discipline over aggression. For fair average quality material, the market is likely to gravitate just slightly below MSP, which remains a natural psychological and commercial anchor. Paying significant premiums above this level in the early arrival phase could expose buyers to mark-to-market risk as mandi inflows deepen and quality improves. Equally important is the focus on mid-quality, commercially workable lots rather than chasing only top-grade material. The current crop shows variability in moisture and grain uniformity, but for most processors and traders, well-cleaned mid-quality chana offers the best balance between cost, recovery, and inventory risk. High-moisture lots should continue to attract

appropriate discounts, while exceptionally premium lots may remain thin in volume and expensive to accumulate at scale. For every 1% moisture discount roughly ₹40–₹60/qtl equivalent. With arrivals expected to accelerate through March and April, the probability of any sharp upside across the season appears limited. The market today is not signalling scarcity; it is signalling abundance with quality differentiation. Buyers who front-load procurement too early risk locking in inventory at levels that may look expensive once the full crop pressure comes to market. This arrival phase offers the government a timely window to build buffer stocks in a calibrated manner. With fair-quality chana likely to trade slightly below MSP amid rising arrivals, procurement now can support farmer realisations without fuelling retail inflation. The prudent approach, therefore, is clear: build coverage gradually, stay anchored slightly below MSP for fair quality, and remain flexible as arrival momentum strengthens. In the current chana cycle, success will not come from chasing the first rally—but from patient accumulation aligned with the flow of the crop.
